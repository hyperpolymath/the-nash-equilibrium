== Overview

The Nash Equilibrium is designed as both entertainment and educational tool.
This document maps game mechanics to learning outcomes and real-world parallels.

== Learning Objectives

=== Primary Objectives

. *Systems thinking* — understand how interconnected systems (economic,
  environmental, social) interact and create emergent behaviour
. *Sustainability science* — learn the planetary boundaries framework and
  why staying within safe operating spaces matters
. *Economic literacy* — compare capitalism, socialism, green economics, and
  techno-utopian models through direct experience
. *Game theory* — internalise concepts like Nash Equilibrium, Prisoner's
  Dilemma, and Tragedy of the Commons through gameplay

=== Secondary Objectives

. *Historical awareness* — connect game eras to real historical transitions
. *Strategic planning* — develop long-term thinking under uncertainty
. *Negotiation skills* — practice trade, diplomacy, and coalition-building
. *Ethical reasoning* — confront trade-offs between growth and sustainability

== Real-World Parallels

=== Tragedy of the Commons (Hardin, 1968)

*Game mechanic:* Shared resource hexes that any player can exploit. Over-
exploitation depletes the resource for everyone.

*Real-world parallel:* Overfishing, deforestation, atmospheric CO2 as a shared
sink, antibiotic resistance.

*Learning outcome:* Individual rational behaviour can lead to collective
irrational outcomes. Cooperation and governance structures are needed to
manage shared resources.

=== Prisoner's Dilemma

*Game mechanic:* Crisis cards where players secretly choose to cooperate or
defect. Mutual cooperation yields the best collective outcome, but defection
tempts with short-term advantage.

*Real-world parallel:* International climate negotiations, arms races, trade
wars, vaccine distribution.

*Learning outcome:* Trust and repeated interaction change optimal strategies.
One-shot defection may win short-term but destroys long-term cooperation.

=== Nash Equilibrium

*Game mechanic:* The meta-strategic dynamic where players seek optimal
strategies given what others are doing. No player can improve by unilaterally
changing strategy at equilibrium.

*Real-world parallel:* Market competition, international diplomacy, evolutionary
stable strategies, OPEC production decisions.

*Learning outcome:* Optimal individual strategy depends on the strategies of
others. Pure domination is rarely stable; finding equilibrium is key.

=== Externalities

*Game mechanic:* Pollution tokens from factories affect all players through
planetary boundary stress, not just the polluter.

*Real-world parallel:* Industrial pollution, carbon emissions, plastic waste,
agricultural runoff.

*Learning outcome:* Costs of production are often borne by society, not the
producer. Regulation and pricing mechanisms can internalise these costs.

=== Tipping Points and Cascade Failure

*Game mechanic:* Boundary stress above 8 triggers automatic cascade to linked
boundaries. Once a boundary reaches 10 (Black), it is irreversible.

*Real-world parallel:* Arctic ice loss -> albedo feedback, Amazon dieback ->
carbon release, coral reef collapse -> marine food chain disruption.

*Learning outcome:* Earth systems have nonlinear dynamics — gradual pressure
can lead to sudden, irreversible change. Prevention is easier than cure.

=== Creative Destruction (Schumpeter)

*Game mechanic:* Era transitions obsolete previous infrastructure. Industrial
factories replace agrarian farms; digital automation replaces industrial labour.

*Real-world parallel:* Horse-drawn carriage -> automobile, typewriter -> computer,
fossil fuels -> renewables (in progress).

*Learning outcome:* Economic progress destroys old industries while creating new
ones. Managing this transition is a core political and social challenge.

=== Doughnut Economics (Raworth, 2017)

*Game mechanic:* The entire planetary boundary + social foundation structure
mirrors Raworth's doughnut model — a safe and just space between ecological
ceiling and social foundation.

*Real-world parallel:* Amsterdam's adoption of doughnut economics as city policy,
UN Sustainable Development Goals.

*Learning outcome:* A thriving economy operates between the social foundation
(minimum human needs) and the ecological ceiling (planetary boundaries).
