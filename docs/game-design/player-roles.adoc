= Player Roles, Characters, and Asymmetric Gameplay
:toc:
:icons: font

== Overview

Players assume asymmetric characters with unique starting bonuses, constraints,
and victory paths. This encourages diverse strategies and highlights tensions
between different economic philosophies.

A character is *not* a fixed role card. Per DD-006, it is built in layers: an
immutable *Core* that everyone can see, a set of *Aspects* that inflect it and
stay hidden, a *name* the player supplies at the table, and a hidden *private
objective* running alongside the main game.

[NOTE]
====
*Status: framework, not final content.* The layer structure and the design rules
below are settled (DD-006, DD-013). The aspect pools and private-objective deck
are illustrative first drafts — Phase 1 work, pending playtesting.
====

== 1. The Character Model

[cols="1,1,3", options="header"]
|===
| Layer | Visibility | Content

| *Core*
| *Public*
| The archetypal essence — a *type*, never a person. Immutable. Sets the
  character's fundamental strengths, constraints, and victory path.

| *Aspects*
| *Hidden*
| Two strengths, one weakness, and one appeal/antipathy pair, drawn or chosen
  from the Core's pools. Inflects *how* this instance of the Core plays.

| *Name*
| Player's own
| Supplied at the table. Never printed. See §6.

| *Private objective*
| *Hidden*
| A quiet parallel goal. Non-critical to winning, but tugs against the commons.
  See §5.
|===

=== Why the Core is public and the rest is not

Per DD-013, this split is deliberate. Public Cores keep the table *legible* —
everyone can see you are a techno-plutocrat, and can bargain with you
accordingly, which the eXchange pillar (DD-011) requires. Hidden aspects and
objectives mean nobody knows your exact payoff function, only a distribution over
it — which is what makes the game one of incomplete information, and its
equilibrium a *Bayes–Nash* one.

Legibility on the outside, uncertainty underneath.

Deciding what to *reveal*, and when, is itself a move: disclosing a strength to
win a negotiation is a costly signal. The exact mechanism (voluntary, triggered,
or costed) is open — see the DD-013 tracker entry.

== 2. The Four Cores

Each Core is a *category* of actor, not a portrait of an individual.

[cols="1,2,3", options="header"]
|===
| Core | Archetype | Immutable essence (public)

| *The Chartered Extractor*
| Colonial Capitalist
| Wealth extracted from distant territories under a licence from an authority
  back home. Rich, dirty, and living on borrowed legitimacy.

| *The Nonviolent Mobiliser*
| Cooperative Socialist
| Moves people, not armies. Social cohesion over growth rate. Cannot compel —
  only persuade.

| *The Off-World Plutocrat*
| Techno-Utopian Innovator
| Vast private capital and a cult following that grants reach far beyond any
  formal power. Fixated on an exit — off this world entirely. Better at
  conjuring belief than at governing or complying.

| *The Steward*
| Environmental Steward
| Protects the boundaries everyone else is drawing down. Capital-poor,
  time-rich; weak early, indispensable late.
|===

== 3. Presets (Beginner Mode)

A preset is a *fully-built character*: Core plus aspects pre-chosen, with a
suggested name offered at the table. Deal one and play — no build step. Per
DD-008, presets are the low-complexity end of the same system, not a separate
ruleset.

The four presets below are the original role cards, unchanged mechanically.

=== Preset: "Queen Isabella" — The Chartered Extractor

[cols="1,3"]
|===
| Starting Bonuses | +2 Gold/turn (colonial trade routes)
| Constraints | Cannot use barter economy; high base pollution rate
| Starting Era Bonus | Strong in Industrial era (colonial wealth)
| Weakness | Vulnerable to independence movements and climate crises
| Path to Victory | Exploit -> Industrialise -> Adapt (or face revolution)
|===

*Flavour:* Rapid wealth extraction from new territories at the cost of indigenous
populations and environmental degradation. Must transition to sustainable
practices before boundaries collapse.

*Parallels:* Spanish Empire, chartered trading companies, extraction economies.

=== Preset: "Gandhi" — The Nonviolent Mobiliser

[cols="1,3"]
|===
| Starting Bonuses | +1 Stability/turn (nonviolent resistance, social cohesion)
| Constraints | Cannot build military units; growth rate halved
| Starting Era Bonus | Strong in Agrarian era (community-based)
| Weakness | Slow expansion; vulnerable to military-capable players
| Path to Victory | Sustainability -> Equality -> Collective advancement
|===

*Flavour:* Non-violent, community-centred development. Strong social fabric but
slow economic growth. Wins through collective well-being rather than individual
dominance.

*Parallels:* Independence movements, cooperative movements, social democracy.

=== Preset: "Elon Musk" — The Off-World Plutocrat

[cols="1,3"]
|===
| Starting Bonuses | +1 Knowledge/turn (innovation engine)
| Constraints | High Knowledge cost for breakthroughs; Labour instability
| Starting Era Bonus | Strong in Digital era (tech disruption)
| Weakness | Social instability from automation; expensive research
| Path to Victory | Automate -> Breakthrough -> Space (Dyson Sphere)
|===

*Flavour:* Technology as the solution to all problems. Powerful innovation engine
that creates social disruption through automation, and can neglect environmental
boundaries in pursuit of breakthroughs.

*Parallels:* Tech industry, private space programmes, nuclear optimism.

=== Preset: "Green Activist" — The Steward

[cols="1,3"]
|===
| Starting Bonuses | +1 Nature/turn (conservation, ecosystem restoration)
| Constraints | Low starting Capital; vulnerable to economic exploitation
| Starting Era Bonus | Weak early, strong in Post-Scarcity era
| Weakness | Economic disadvantage in Industrial/Digital eras
| Path to Victory | Balance -> Resilience -> Post-Scarcity (sustainable)
|===

*Flavour:* Protects planetary boundaries but starts economically weak. Becomes
powerful in later eras as boundary stress makes sustainability essential.

*Parallels:* Environmental movements, doughnut economics, degrowth.

== 4. The Builder (Advanced Mode)

Take a Core, then draw or choose:

* *2 Strengths* from that Core's strength pool
* *1 Weakness* from its weakness pool
* *1 Appeal/Antipathy pair*
* *1 Private objective* (§5)

*Draw* for a fast, surprising game; *choose* for a deliberate one; *draft* (pass
the pool around) for a competitive one. All three are legal — the group picks.

The same Core therefore plays differently every session, and — because aspects
are hidden — your opponents must infer which build they are facing from how you
behave. That inference *is* the game.

=== Worked example: The Off-World Plutocrat

Illustrative pools, to show the shape. Numbers are placeholders pending Phase 1.

*Strengths (draw/choose 2):*

[cols="1,3"]
|===
| Hype Engine | Once per turn, convert Stability into Capital — belief as a resource.
| Vertical Integration | Ignore one input prerequisite per Leontief recipe.
| Regulatory Arbitrage | Relocate to dodge one crisis effect per era.
| Talent Magnet | +1 Knowledge whenever *any* player completes research.
|===

*Weaknesses (draw/choose 1):*

[cols="1,3"]
|===
| Thin Skin | Lose Stability whenever publicly defected against.
| Attention Deficit | Cannot hold more than two binding agreements at once.
| Overpromise | Must announce a breakthrough one turn before it resolves.
|===

*Appeal / Antipathy (draw/choose 1 pair):*

[cols="1,3"]
|===
| Retail believers / Regulators | Bonus while Stability is high; penalised in dealings with institutional players.
| Engineers / Labour | Bonus to research trades; penalised on Labour stability.
|===

Note that these inflect *the same public Core*. Everyone can see you are an
off-world plutocrat. Nobody can see whether you are the thin-skinned kind who
must be handled gently, or the arbitrage kind who will simply leave.

== 5. Private Objectives

A hidden parallel goal, drawn at setup. Non-critical — you can win without it,
and it should never overshadow the main game — but it adds challenge, and it
makes your true payoff function unreadable.

[IMPORTANT]
====
*Design rule: private objectives must tug against the commons — never run
orthogonal to it.*

The shared planetary-boundaries dashboard (DD-007) is the game's commons
dilemma. A private objective is *interesting* exactly when it tempts you to
defect from it on a given turn: _do I spend this action shoring up the boundary,
or quietly advancing my own exit?_

An objective *orthogonal* to the commons is a solitaire task that pulls attention
off the table. An objective *incentive-misaligned* with it deepens the very
dilemma the game is about.

*Misaligned, never independent.* Every objective drafted in Phase 1 must be
checked against this rule.
====

*Example — "Escape Hatch"* (Off-World Plutocrat): quietly bank Capital and
Knowledge off-world. Scores *even if the commons collapses* — but every unit
banked is a unit not spent on the boundary. This is the rule made concrete: the
objective pays best precisely when you defect, and the table cannot see you doing
it — only infer it from what you decline to contribute.

== 6. Naming at the Table

The name is the *player's*, always. Nothing on the printed card names a real
person.

*The line to hold — archetype, not portrait.* Printed Cores describe a
*category* of actor. They must stay free of uniquely-identifying specifics — real
company names, real quotations, one-to-one biography — that would make a Core
identifiable as one particular living individual. "A Mars-fixated plutocrat with
a cult following" is a *type*; a great many people would recognise several
candidates, and that is exactly the point.

This is not a limitation on the humour — it is what *licenses* it. We hand the
players a caricature of a type, and the players do the impression. The game makes
no claim about any real person.

The Day of the Triffids is never held to a standard of validity, reliability, and

== 7. Expansion Cores (Future)

* *The Monument Builder* — Agrarian era specialist, monumental construction
* *The Revolutionary* — can trigger regime change in other players
* *The Corsair* — disrupts trade routes, asymmetric warfare
* *The Elder* — deep Nature connection, unique boundary recovery abilities
* *The Singleton* — Digital era specialist, post-human victory path

== 8. Balance Notes

* Cores are intentionally *not* balanced for raw power — they are balanced for
  *interesting decisions*.
* Each Core has a window of strength and a window of vulnerability.
* The Nash Equilibrium dynamic means no single Core can win alone in most games.
* Aspects must not stack into a dominant build. Phase 1 balance critics should
  Monte-Carlo the aspect pools for runaway combinations before any playtest is
  spent discovering them.
* Playtesting should confirm each Core has meaningful agency in every era, and
  that hidden aspects create *inference*, not *confusion*.
