= Mechanism Survey: Mappings to Engelstein & Shalev
:toc:
:icons: font

== Purpose

This document maps twenty-five mechanism entries from Engelstein and Shalev's
_Building Blocks of Tabletop Game Design_ (2nd ed., 2022) onto the four
economic principles that anchor _The Nash Equilibrium_. The survey is a
*design reference*, not canonical theory; the load-bearing specification is
`economic-foundations.adoc`. The defection ledger and trade-card library are
specified in `trade-cards.adoc`, the Turn 12 walkthrough is in
`gameplay-storyboard.adoc`.

The entries here are organised by *which Nash Equilibrium principle they
serve* rather than by Engelstein's chapters. Same content, different axis.
Where Engelstein groups by mechanism family (Actions, Economics, Uncertainty,
…), we group by load-bearing role in our own design — making the survey a
checklist for design decisions rather than a wiki of someone else's
taxonomy.

== Reading Conventions

Each entry is cited as `ID Name (book p.N)`, where the ID is Engelstein's
stable mnemonic (e.g. `ACT-10`, `ECO-15`) and the page reference is the
printed book page (CRC Press, 2022).

Each entry carries one of three verdicts:

[cols="1,3"]
|===
| *ADOPT* | Engelstein's mechanism is taken essentially as described, modulo
   thematic skinning. Our existing design already uses or should use it.
| *ADAPT* | The mechanism is taken with material modification to fit one of
   our load-bearing constraints (Leontief recipes, emergent stability,
   public engine state, four-factor model).
| *REJECT* | The mechanism is incompatible with one of our locked-in
   decisions and is *not* used. Documented here only to forestall
   re-litigation.
|===

The survey is selective. Engelstein covers ~250 mechanisms across thirteen
chapters; this document treats the twenty-five most directly relevant to our
four economic principles, our 6X pillar structure, and the Scythe-style
paired action grid currently under consideration.

== Principle 1 — Scarcity and Opportunity Cost

These mechanisms make foregone alternatives *visible*. Robbins's (1932)
opportunity-cost framing is mechanically realised when a player's choice of
one action geometrically forecloses others.

=== ACT-02 Action Drafting (book p.81)

*Mechanism.* Players select Actions from a shared pool; once selected, an
Action is denied to other players. Worker Placement and Role Selection are
sub-types.

*Mapping.* Implements opportunity cost at the *inter-player* layer:
choosing locks competitors out of the same Action this round.

*Verdict.* ADAPT. Our Scythe-style paired action grid is closer to ACT-10
Rondel + ACT-11 Selection Restrictions than to pure drafting; columns are
not denied to other players, only to *the same player on the next turn*.

*Example.* _Citadels_ (Faidutti, 2000); _Puerto Rico_ (Seyfarth, 2002).

=== ACT-10 Rondel (book p.102)

*Mechanism.* Available Actions are arranged on a circle. Each player has a
token; on their turn they advance it and take the Action where it lands.
Moving further is more costly.

*Mapping.* Geometric realisation of opportunity cost: the player sees
exactly which Actions they are passing over, and at what cost.

*Verdict.* ADAPT. Our paired-action grid borrows the Rondel's *spatial
inevitability* (you cannot pretend the foregone option doesn't exist) but
replaces the circle with three columns of paired top/bottom actions.

*Example.* _Imperial_ (Gerdts, 2006); _Concordia_ (Gerdts, 2013).

=== ACT-11 Action Selection Restrictions (book p.104)

*Mechanism.* A constraint on which Actions a player may choose this turn.
Engelstein collects exotic instances: Kemet's pyramid, Goa's grid adjacency,
Tzolk'in's rotating gears, Keyforge's house restriction.

*Mapping.* The restriction is the opportunity-cost mechanism — it forces
foregone alternatives into the present decision.

*Verdict.* ADOPT. The Scythe no-repeat-column rule belongs to this family.
Cannot pick the same column two turns running. One sentence in the rules,
massive effect on play: prevents production-spam, prevents single-pillar
dominance.

*Example.* _Keyforge: Call of the Archons_ (Garfield, 2018); _Tzolk'in_
(Luciani and Tascini, 2012).

=== ECO-17 Increase Value of Unchosen Resources (book p.345)

*Mechanism.* Actions or Resources not chosen this turn accumulate value
(coins, bonus points) until someone takes them. _Puerto Rico_'s unfilled
roles are the canonical case.

*Mapping.* Surfaces opportunity cost by making *delay* the price.

*Verdict.* REJECT. Incompatible with the Leontief recipe model: recipes are
binary (fire / idle), not Dutch-auction-priced. Adding accumulating coins to
unfired recipes would convert deterministic production into a price-discovery
mechanism, which is exactly what our model rejects in favour of Hayekian
emergence (Foundations §7).

*Example.* _Puerto Rico_ (Seyfarth, 2002); _Small World_ (Keyaerts, 2009).

== Principle 2 — Information Symmetry and Fog

Our commitment is *strong*: the entire economic engine (recipes,
infrastructure, defection ledger, dashboard) is public; fog applies only to
tactical hex contents. Engelstein's hidden-information mechanisms are
mostly incompatible with this; the few that survive are softer asymmetries.

=== UNC-05 Roles with Asymmetric Information (book p.280)

*Mechanism.* One player has different information from the others — the
"spy" doesn't know the location, the "fake artist" doesn't know the word.
Often one role is opposed to the rest.

*Mapping.* Tempting for pathway asymmetry, but the strong form (one player
hides identity or reads a different game) violates our public-engine
commitment.

*Verdict.* ADAPT. The *weak* form survives: pathway players read the
dashboard with different *priorities*, not different *data*. The Green
Activist sees high Climate stress as proximate-loss; Queen Isabella sees the
same data as a tradeable carbon offset opportunity. Same numbers, different
salience. This is asymmetry of *valuation*, not of information.

*Example.* _Spyfall_ (Ushan, 2014) — rejected pattern. _Mysterium_ (Nevskiy
and Sidorenko, 2015) — rejected pattern. _Hanabi_ (Bauza, 2010) — partial
analogue (information asymmetric in form, public in substance).

=== UNC-09 Probability Management (book p.290)

*Mechanism.* Players cannot directly determine outcomes but can shift the
*probabilities* of outcomes — buying dice modifiers, deck-thinning,
managing the composition of a draw deck.

*Mapping.* Would attach to Random Production (ECO-12) or dice-based combat,
neither of which we use.

*Verdict.* REJECT. Our recipes are deterministic; production is binary
activation, not stochastic. We have no draw deck whose composition the
player can tune. Probability Management requires randomness somewhere
load-bearing in the economy, and we have deliberately removed it.

*Example.* _Dominion_ (Vaccarino, 2008); _Twilight Struggle_ (Gupta and
Matthews, 2005).

== Principle 3 — Factors and Factor Returns

The largest cluster. These mechanisms operationalise factor flows
(Land→Rent, Labour→Wage, Entrepreneurship→Profit, Capital→Interest), the
Leontief recipe model, and the endogenous-growth dynamics that turn factors
into accumulating productivity.

=== ECO-01 Exchanging (book p.308)

*Mechanism.* A fixed-formula transformation of one set of assets into
another, via the bank. Distinguishes hierarchical from lateral conversions.

*Mapping.* Our Leontief recipe firings are exactly hierarchical Exchanges:
fixed input vector → fixed output vector. Each recipe tile *is* an
ECO-01 transformation.

*Verdict.* ADOPT. Recipe tiles are the canonical visual realisation of
ECO-01. Cross-reference: `core-mechanics.adoc` Resources section.

*Example.* _Roads and Boats_ (Doumen and Wiersinga, 1999) — particularly
relevant: hierarchical resource graph similar to our Energy → Capital flow.

=== ECO-02 Trading (book p.310)

*Mechanism.* Players Exchange directly with each other; terms are
negotiated, not formula-fixed. Set Collection (Chapter 12) is the typical
mechanism for ensuring resources have different values to different players.

*Mapping.* Our typed trade cards (Debt-for-Nature Swap, Carbon Offset
Purchase, Long-Term Supply Contract) are constrained-form Trading: the
*template* is fixed, the *terms* are negotiated within it.

*Verdict.* ADOPT. The constrained-template form solves the open-trading
downsides Engelstein flags (slow, excludes shy players) without losing
strategic depth. Cross-reference: `trade-cards.adoc`.

*Example.* _Sidereal Confluence_ (Deichmann, 2017); _Civilization_
(Tresham, 1980).

=== ECO-05 Income (book p.318)

*Mechanism.* Players gain resources at defined times — scheduled,
player-controlled, or random. Open vs Closed economies.

*Mapping.* Factor returns (Rent / Wage / Profit / Interest) are our Income
mechanism. Each accrues from a different factor: Land yields Rent each turn,
Labour yields Wage when employed in a recipe, Capital yields Interest as
infrastructure throughput, Entrepreneurship yields Profit when innovation
succeeds.

*Verdict.* ADAPT. We use scheduled Income (every turn factors are owned)
*plus* player-controlled Income (recipe firings). We are explicitly an Open
Economy with the Pigouvian dashboard tracking the externalities the open
economy generates.

*Example.* _Brass_ (Wallace, 2007); _Le Havre_ (Rosenberg, 2008) — both
combine scheduled and player-controlled income.

=== ECO-13 Investment (book p.335)

*Mechanism.* Share ownership in companies; dividends and majority bonuses.
Most prominently in 18xx games.

*Mapping.* Could in principle be used for joint stewardship of Nature
reserves or shared infrastructure, but the corporate-share metaphor is the
mechanism's heart.

*Verdict.* REJECT. Inserts a corporate-finance layer between players and
factors that has no analogue in our four-factor model. Pathways are
philosophical archetypes, not corporations. Adopting share-ownership would
require a fifth factor (financial capital separate from physical capital)
which we have explicitly excluded.

*Example.* _1830_ (Tresham, 1986); _Acquire_ (Sackson, 1964).

=== ECO-14 Ownership (book p.338)

*Mechanism.* Players own entities; can use them freely; collect a fee when
others use them.

*Mapping.* This is *Land* in the Ricardian sense. A player who owns a hex
(through eXpand) extracts Rent when others traverse or use it.

*Verdict.* ADOPT. Land ownership generates Rent flow exactly per ECO-14.
Trade Posts owned by player A but used by player B's recipes pay Rent to A.
This is the cleanest mechanical implementation of the Land-factor return in
classical economics.

*Example.* _Le Havre_ (Rosenberg, 2008); _Caylus_ (Attia, 2005).

=== ECO-15 Contracts (book p.341)

*Mechanism.* Players fulfil pre-defined Contracts (specific input
combination → specific reward). Contracts may be public (race) or private
(only the holder may complete).

*Mapping.* Our typed trade cards include Contract-shaped instances:
Long-Term Supply Contract, Carbon Offset Purchase, Royalty/Concession.

*Verdict.* ADOPT. Engelstein's binding-Contract framing is exactly what
distinguishes our trade cards from informal Negotiation (ECO-18). Pair with
the Defection Ledger to enforce contracts that span multiple turns.

*Example.* _Empire Builder_ (Bromley and Fawcett, 1982); _Century: Spice
Road_ (Matsuuchi, 2017).

=== ECO-19 Alliances (book p.350)

*Mechanism.* Formal multi-player relationships with rules-defined effects:
allied players cannot attack each other, may share scoring, may break the
alliance at defined moments.

*Mapping.* The Collective Emissions Pact (`trade-cards.adoc`) is a
multilateral ECO-19. Pathway-based loose alliances (Green Activist + Gandhi
on conservation) are emergent ECO-19s without formal cards.

*Verdict.* ADOPT. The defection-ledger is what makes Alliances binding
across turns. Engelstein notes that _Cosmic Encounter_'s alliance step is
short-lived (one turn); ours are explicitly multi-turn with formal exit
penalties.

*Example.* _Dune_ (Eberle, Kittredge, and Olatka, 1979); _Cosmic Encounter_
(Eberle et al., 1977).

=== ECO-20 Resource Queue (book p.352)

*Mechanism.* Resources are arranged in a line; only the head (or tail) can
be drawn from. Walks the Dogs, Patchwork, Bohnanza.

*Mapping.* Could in principle be used for crisis-card reveal order or for
ordered recipe firing, but neither use is well-motivated.

*Verdict.* REJECT. Recipes fire in *parallel* (any subset of recipes whose
inputs are available may fire); a head-of-queue restriction would convert
this into serial production, which contradicts the simultaneous-economy
character of the game. Crisis cards are dashboard-driven (boundary-stress
triggered), not queue-positioned.

*Example.* _Patchwork_ (Rosenberg, 2014) — well-implemented Resource Queue
that we are choosing not to emulate.

=== WPL-05 Adding and Blocking Buildings (book p.417)

*Mechanism.* Buildings (action spaces) are added to the worker-placement
pool during play; some are public, some are owned. Soft blocking, bumping,
and blocked-by-cost variants exist.

*Mapping.* Recipe tiles are buildings. They are added to the player's
tableau through eXpand and eXperiment, and used through eXploit.

*Verdict.* ADAPT. We borrow the *adding* dimension fully but mostly
*reject blocking*: a recipe can fire each turn for its owner, and other
players can only access it through trade or alliance, not through
worker-placement competition. This keeps recipes in the production-economy
domain rather than turning them into action-drafting spaces.

*Example.* _Caylus_ (Attia, 2005); _Lords of Waterdeep_ (Lee and Thompson,
2012).

=== ACT-15 Gating and Unlocking (book p.112)

*Mechanism.* New Actions become available when conditions trigger — turn
count, threshold crossed, shared resource depleted, narrative beat reached.

*Mapping.* Era progression (Agrarian → Industrial → Digital → Post-Scarcity)
is gating by turn count. Pathway-specific recipe unlocks are gating by
pathway-progress threshold. Boundary-cascade-triggered crisis cards are
gating by dashboard state.

*Verdict.* ADOPT. We use ACT-15 in three load-bearing places. The mechanism
is general enough to bind era progression, pathway progression, and crisis
escalation under one design.

*Example.* _Through the Ages_ (Chvátil, 2006); _SpaceCorp_ (Butterfield,
2018) — both era-gated.

=== ACT-16 Tech Trees / Tech Tracks / Track Bonuses (book p.115)

*Mechanism.* Per-player Action upgrades, typically bought with a research
currency. Subtly different from ACT-15: gating is global, tech is personal.

*Mapping.* Pathway-progress trackers are Tech Tracks: each pathway
(Capitalist / Socialist / Green / Techno-Utopian) has milestone bonuses
unlocked by pathway-specific actions.

*Verdict.* ADAPT. The full *tech-tree* form (research prerequisites,
Eclipse-style branches) is more complexity than we want; we use the
flatter *tech-track* form (Russian Railroads, Orleans), where progress is
linear and milestones grant fixed bonuses. The eXperiment pillar feeds the
track but does not branch it.

*Example.* _Russian Railroads_ (Ohley and Orgler, 2013); _Orleans_
(Stockhausen, 2014).

=== ACT-20 Layering (Splaying subset) (book p.125)

*Mechanism.* Components placed atop other components; only top-most icons
active. The *Splaying* subset (Innovation, Mystic Vale) keeps cards layered
exactly so that previously-covered icons can be revealed by re-arrangement.

*Mapping.* Scythe's engine-reveal is a hard-card analogue: as you place
infrastructure tokens onto the main board, you uncover discount slots
underneath. Our recipe-tile placement on the player mat could similarly
uncover efficiency bonuses (reduced Knowledge cost on Lab firings, etc.).

*Verdict.* ADOPT (Splaying subset only). The general Layering mechanism
(stacked tableau, transparent cards) is more component-heavy than we want.
Splaying — covered slots that reveal bonuses on uncovering — is the
Solow/Romer growth mechanic made tactile, and is the highest-leverage single
borrowing from this entire survey.

*Example.* _Innovation_ (Chudyk, 2010); _Mystic Vale_ (Clair, 2016);
_Scythe_ (Stegmaier, 2016).

=== SET-04 Network Building (book p.519)

*Mechanism.* Sets-as-edges-between-nodes; routes through a graph that
provides synergy beyond the sum of parts. Three sub-types: Point-to-Point,
Tile Placement, Existing Network.

*Mapping.* Trade routes between player territories form a network. Trade
cards (Most-Favoured-Nation Clause, Long-Term Supply Contract) operate
across network edges. The defection ledger weights edges with trust.

*Verdict.* ADOPT. We use the Existing Network sub-type (Ticket to Ride,
Power Grid): the hex map's territories are pre-defined, players claim
edges through the eXchange pillar.

*Example.* _Ticket to Ride_ (Moon, 2004); _Power Grid_ (Friese, 2004);
_Brass_ (Wallace, 2007).

== Principle 4 — Externalities

The boundary dashboard is a Pigouvian register of externalised costs. The
relevant Engelstein mechanism is the trigger that converts accumulated
externalised cost into game-ending consequence.

=== VIC-15 Circuit Breaker / Sudden Death (book p.244)

*Mechanism.* A fixed end condition plus a *special* condition that ends the
game prematurely — typically when one player runs away with it
(Circuit Breaker) or when a special victory is achieved (Sudden Death).

*Mapping.* Boundary collapse is a Circuit Breaker, but inverted: it ends
the game in *cooperative loss* rather than individual victory. If any of
the nine boundary tracks reaches Black (10), the game ends and *no player
wins*. This converts the externality dashboard from a passive scoreboard
into an active end-state trigger.

*Verdict.* ADOPT. This is the mechanism that makes externalities
*economically real* in our design. Without VIC-15, the dashboard is
ornamental; with it, every recipe's pollution-token output is a marginal
contribution to a known catastrophe threshold.

*Example.* _Twilight Struggle_ (Gupta and Matthews, 2005) — DEFCON 1
triggers immediate loss for whoever caused it; structurally identical to
our boundary-cascade trigger except cooperative rather than individual.

== Meta-Design — Game Structure, Endgame, and Strategic Tension

These mechanisms shape the *texture* of strategic interaction rather than
serving any single principle. They are why the game has its characteristic
shape.

=== STR-05 Semi-Cooperative Games (book p.15)

*Mechanism.* Players win or lose individually, *and* win or lose
collectively. The collective loss condition is necessary; without it, the
game is just a competitive game with negotiation.

*Mapping.* The boundary-cascade collective loss + per-pathway individual
victory makes us a textbook semi-cooperative game in Engelstein's
definition.

*Verdict.* ADOPT. Engelstein names the failure mode honestly ("like trying
to land a jet plane in a phone booth") and the prerequisite ("all players
must agree whether group-win-with-individual-loss is acceptable"). Our
design must communicate this expectation clearly in the rulebook —
otherwise sabotage and grief are predictable.

*Example.* _Republic of Rome_ (Berthold, Greenwood, and Haines, 1990);
_CO2_ (Lacerda, 2012); _Archipelago_ (Boelinger, 2012).

=== VIC-19 Tug of War (book p.252)

*Mechanism.* A marker moves up and back along a track between two opposed
endpoints. Reaching an end triggers victory or scoring.

*Mapping.* Each boundary track on the dashboard is a *single-direction*
Tug of War — only one end (Black/collapse) is a trigger; the other end
(Green/safe) is a passive state. Restoration efforts pull the marker back
toward Green; production externalities push it toward Black.

*Verdict.* ADAPT. We are using VIC-19 inverted (single-direction, against
the human players collectively) rather than two-player. Each of the nine
boundaries is its own track; cascade arrows allow stress to spill between
them.

*Example.* _Twilight Struggle_'s VP track is the canonical
two-player Tug of War. _Churchill_'s three-player track is closer to our
multi-track form.

=== RES-08 Prisoner's Dilemma (book p.159)

*Mechanism.* Two-or-more-player choice between Cooperate and Defect, where
mutual cooperation maximises total payoff but unilateral defection
maximises individual payoff.

*Mapping.* This is the *theoretical core* of our entire game. The name
"Nash Equilibrium" announces it. Every trade card is a potential PD;
the defection ledger formalises the iterated-PD tit-for-tat penalty
structure.

*Verdict.* ADOPT, with strong endorsement. Engelstein's caveat — that
two-player PD always has Defect as the dominant strategy — is exactly why
we have four players: PD only generates interesting dynamics with three or
more, where the *third party*'s reaction creates iterated stakes.

*Example.* _Diplomacy_ (Calhamer, 1959); _Cosmic Encounter_ (Eberle et al.,
1977) — both feature non-binding negotiation that becomes binding through
reputation effects, exactly our defection-ledger pattern.

=== ECO-15 Contracts and ECO-18 Negotiation (book pp.341, 348)

*Mechanism (ECO-18).* Players make agreements about future actions, often
non-binding, often distinct from resource-trading.

*Mapping.* The *binding-vs-non-binding* distinction Engelstein draws is
load-bearing for our trade-card design. Carbon Offset Purchase (binding
ECO-15) and the Tariff Wall declaration (non-binding ECO-18) are
deliberately mechanically distinct.

*Verdict.* ADOPT both. The defection ledger turns nominally non-binding
ECO-18 negotiations into *socially binding* commitments, because reneging
costs future trust capital. This is the iterated-game embedding of
Coase (1960) bargaining into a finite-horizon design.

*Example.* _Diplomacy_ — non-binding promises (ECO-18) with reputational
consequences. _The Resistance_ (Eskridge, 2009) — voting-as-implicit-
negotiation.

== Pathway Asymmetry

=== ACT-12 Variable Player Powers (book p.106)

*Mechanism.* Each player has special Actions or modifiers unique to them.
Engelstein notes the cognitive-load tradeoff: simple framework + diverse
powers (Cosmic Encounter) vs complex framework + matched powers (Root,
Spirit Island).

*Mapping.* Queen Isabella, Gandhi, Elon Musk, Green Activist are
Variable Player Powers in Engelstein's strict sense — distinct starting
bonuses, constraints, and victory paths.

*Verdict.* ADOPT. Engelstein's design caution is highly relevant to us:
keep the *base* mechanics simple (recipe firings, dashboard, ledger)
precisely so that the asymmetric pathway powers can carry weight. Cosmic
Encounter's "simple combat resolution + radical alien powers" is the
explicit model.

*Example.* _Cosmic Encounter_ (Eberle et al., 1977); _Root_ (Wehrle, 2018);
_Spirit Island_ (Reuss, 2017).

== Cross-Cutting Recommendations

The twenty-five entries cluster into five design moves, each more
load-bearing than any single entry:

*1. The "engine-reveal" cluster: ACT-15 + ACT-16 + ACT-20 (Splaying) + WPL-05.*
These four mechanisms span four chapters of Engelstein but implement the
same economic idea — *endogenous productivity growth* — at different
granularities. ACT-15 is global gating (era transition unlocks). ACT-16 is
per-player track progression (pathway milestones). ACT-20 Splaying is the
moment-to-moment reveal (build a Lab → uncover its discount). WPL-05 is the
addition of new buildings to the action pool. Recognising this clustering
collapses what looked like four separate decisions into one design move:
the Splaying form is the *load-bearing* expression because it is tactile
(removing a token reveals a bonus) and self-documenting (the player's mat
visibly grows in capability without rule-text).
+
The recommendation is to commit to Splaying for in-construction recipes,
ACT-16 tracks for pathway progression, and ACT-15 gating for era
transitions — three layers of the same economic principle, each at its
appropriate temporal grain.

*2. The "binding-strength ladder" of economic interaction: ECO-01 → ECO-02 → ECO-15 → ECO-18 → ECO-19.*
Engelstein implicitly arranges these in increasing order of how far a
commitment extends in time and how non-formula the terms are: Exchange is
immediate and formula-fixed; Trade is immediate and negotiated; Contract is
deferred but formula-fixed; Negotiation is deferred and non-formula;
Alliance is deferred, structural, and multilateral. Our typed trade-card
library should be cross-mapped to this ladder explicitly:
+
[cols="2,1,3"]
|===
| Trade card | Engelstein rung | Why this rung
| Carbon Offset Purchase | ECO-01 | Immediate, formula-fixed
| Long-Term Supply Contract | ECO-15 | Deferred, formula-fixed
| Royalty/Concession | ECO-15 | Deferred, formula-fixed
| Most-Favoured-Nation Clause | ECO-15 | Procedural Contract on future trades
| Debt-for-Nature Swap | ECO-02 + ECO-15 | Negotiated terms, formula payment
| Tariff Wall | ECO-18 (coercive) | Unilateral, non-binding declaration
| Reparations | ECO-15 (post-coercive) | Formula-fixed, ledger-triggered
| Collective Emissions Pact | ECO-19 | Multilateral, structural
|===
+
Reading the trade cards through this lens makes the *defection ledger*
load-bearing: it is the institution that prevents the deferred rungs
(ECO-15, ECO-18, ECO-19) from collapsing into trivial non-commitment.

*3. The "Scythe-shaped action grid" sits at the intersection of ACT-02 + ACT-10 + ACT-11.*
Engelstein has no single name for the Scythe pattern. It is *drafting*
without denial (you don't lock other players out), it is *rondel*-like
geometric inevitability of foregone alternatives, and it is *selection
restriction* through the no-repeat rule. The combination is greater than
its parts: opportunity cost is realised geometrically (ACT-10), variety is
forced mechanically (ACT-11), and the consumption-vs-investment macro
distinction is realised through the top/bottom action pairing (an
Engelstein-shaped innovation that does not appear under any single ID).
+
The recommendation is to specify the paired action grid in a new doc
(`player-mat-design.adoc`) under three explicit ID citations rather than
trying to name it as a single mechanism.

*4. RES-08 Prisoner's Dilemma is the load-bearing precedent for the entire design.*
Engelstein lists Diplomacy, Cosmic Encounter, Incan Gold, and Dead Last as
exemplars. Our design generalises across all of them by making the PD
*economic*: every trade card is a potential PD, every alliance is a
multi-player PD, every recipe-firing-with-pollution-output is a PD against
the boundary dashboard. The defection ledger formalises Axelrod's
(1984) iterated-PD tit-for-tat memory mechanism.
+
This is the strongest endorsement in the survey. The game's name is
literal, not metaphorical.

*5. The asymmetry recommendation is to pair ACT-12 + weak UNC-05.*
Pathway powers (ACT-12 strict form) plus pathway-specific *valuations* of
the public dashboard (UNC-05 weak form, where data is shared but salience
differs). Engelstein's caution about cognitive load is directly relevant:
keep base mechanics simple precisely so that pathway distinctness can
carry the asymmetric weight without overwhelming new players.

== Tensions and Open Design Questions

The survey surfaced six real tensions with our locked-in design.

. *VIC-15 wants a single trigger; we have nine.* Engelstein's Circuit
Breaker is typically one threshold (Twilight Struggle DEFCON, Liberté
landslide, 7 Wonders Duel military victory). Our boundary collapse is
*any one of nine* tracks reaching Black. Is the dilution of urgency
across nine boundaries a feature (forces multi-front attention) or a bug
(no single boundary feels load-bearing)? Worth thinking about whether some
boundaries should aggregate (e.g. fold biosphere integrity and biogeochemical
flows into a single Living Systems track) so the trigger is sharper.

. *ECO-05 Income's Open vs Closed Economy distinction is unclear in our
design.* We have an Open Economy (Land yields Rent each turn from a bank,
not from other players). But the Pigouvian dashboard is *Closed* in a
specific sense: every pollution token issued by a recipe contributes to a
finite catastrophe budget. This is a Closed *externality* economy on top of
an Open *production* economy. The Foundations doc should probably name
this hybrid explicitly.

. *ECO-14 Ownership's rent-collection mechanic creates "fee creep."*
Engelstein notes that Le Havre and Caylus generate substantial player
interaction through fees-on-others'-buildings, but it slows games. If every
hex Land owner extracts Rent from every traversal, gameplay could grind.
Worth deciding: do we charge Rent on *use* (Le Havre style) or only on
*ownership change* (lighter)?

. *ACT-12 cognitive load risk.* Engelstein names this as the central
design tradeoff: powerful asymmetric powers + simple base = good (Cosmic
Encounter); powerful asymmetric powers + complex base = unteachable. Our
base is *not* simple (recipes, dashboard, ledger, paired action grid, hex
movement). The pathway powers must therefore stay relatively *quiet*
mechanically, expressing themselves through valuation differences rather
than through new actions. Should be named explicitly in the Foundations
doc as a constraint.

. *RES-08 PD requires three-plus players.* Engelstein notes that two-player
PD has Defect as the dominant strategy because there is no third party
whose reaction creates iterated stakes. Our four-player design is fine;
but a *three-player* variant (one role left out) needs careful design
review — at three, defection is still strategically interesting but the
ledger has thinner accounting. A *two-player* variant should probably not
exist.

. *ECO-15 vs ECO-18 enforcement asymmetry.* Engelstein cleanly separates
binding (Contract) from non-binding (Negotiation). Our defection ledger
*partially* binds ECO-18 — reneging costs future trust capital, but
not the immediate transaction. Is this a coherent middle ground or an
unstable one? The honest answer is: untested. Playtesting will tell us
whether non-binding-but-reputation-tracked negotiation feels like cheating
or feels like real diplomacy. This is the single biggest design risk in
the trade-card library.
