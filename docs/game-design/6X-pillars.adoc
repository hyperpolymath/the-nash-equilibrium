= The 6X Pillars
:toc:

== Overview

The Nash Equilibrium extends the classic 4X formula with two additional strategic
dimensions, creating a **6X** strategy game.

== The Six X's

[cols="1,2,4"]
|===
| # | Pillar | Description

| 1 | **eXplore** | Discover the map, uncover resources, find other civilizations
| 2 | **eXpand** | Grow territory, found new settlements, extend influence
| 3 | **eXploit** | Develop resources, build infrastructure, leverage advantages
| 4 | **eXterminate** | Military conflict, elimination of rivals
| 5 | **eXchange** | Trade, diplomacy, alliances, economic interdependence — game-theoretic negotiation where mutual benefit and betrayal coexist
| 6 | **eXperiment** | Research, technology trees, speculative science — invest in uncertain outcomes with asymmetric payoffs
|===

== Why eXchange and eXperiment?

Traditional 4X games treat diplomacy as a thin veneer over military dominance, and
research as a linear tech tree. In The Nash Equilibrium:

**eXchange** is a first-class strategic dimension. Trade agreements, resource
exchanges, and diplomatic pacts are modelled as repeated games where cooperation
and defection have real mechanical consequences. Players must find Nash equilibria
in multi-agent negotiations — or exploit opponents who assume cooperation.

**eXperiment** introduces genuine uncertainty into strategic planning. Research
paths branch non-deterministically. Players invest in speculative technologies
with unknown payoffs, creating information asymmetry that feeds back into the
eXchange pillar.

== The Nash Equilibrium Connection

The game's title reflects its core design principle: every strategic interaction
between players (and AI opponents) can be analysed through the lens of game theory.
The "winning" strategy is rarely pure domination — it's finding the equilibrium
point where your strategy is optimal given what everyone else is doing.
