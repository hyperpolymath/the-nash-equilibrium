== Abstract

_The Nash Equilibrium_ is a 6X strategy board game whose rules are constructed
to encode four foundational principles of economic theory:
(i) scarcity and opportunity cost;
(ii) information symmetry and asymmetry, including fog;
(iii) factors of production and their characteristic returns; and
(iv) externalities. This document specifies the formal economic structure that
underwrites the game's mechanics. We adopt the canonical four-factor production
model (Land, Labour, Entrepreneurship, Capital, with returns Rent, Wage,
Profit, Interest) and reconcile the game's five named gameplay resources
to it, treating Energy as a produced intermediate good in the sense of
Leontief (1941). We argue that the game's stability concept must be _emergent_
in the sense of Hayek (1945) rather than _computed_ in the sense of Walras
(1874) and Arrow & Debreu (1954); the planetary-boundaries dashboard is
specified as a Pigouvian externality register, calibrated against the
Stockholm framework of Rockström et al. (2009). We close with the limitations
of the formalisation and the empirical (playtesting) programme required to
validate it.

== Introduction

The intellectual aspiration of _The Nash Equilibrium_ is that a player who has
played the game well has, _ipso facto_, acted as a competent economic agent
under conditions of scarcity, partial information, factor heterogeneity, and
externalities. The four principles above are not flavour; they are the load-
bearing structure on which the rules are built. This document is intended as
the canonical economic specification for the design — the artefact a future
contributor consults to determine whether a proposed mechanic is consistent
with the game's economic commitments.

Two methodological commitments are made at the outset. First, we follow Robbins
(1932, p.16) in defining economics as "the science which studies human
behaviour as a relationship between ends and scarce means which have
alternative uses"; the game's choice architecture must therefore make scarcity
binding and alternatives visible. Second, we follow Hayek (1945) in treating
equilibrium as a property that markets _reveal_ rather than _compute_; the
game's rules must therefore generate stability signals through the existing
mechanisms (production, exchange, externality registration) rather than
through a separate index layer.

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. <<sec_principles>>
formalises the four principles. <<sec_production>> states the four-factor
production model. <<sec_mapping>> reconciles the five gameplay resources to
the four factors. <<sec_externalities>> develops the boundary dashboard as a
Pigouvian register. <<sec_information>> formalises the fog mechanic.
<<sec_emergence>> defends the Hayekian commitment against the Walrasian
alternative. <<sec_pillars>> binds the production model to the 6X action
pillars. <<sec_limitations>> declares the model's limitations and open
questions.

[[sec_principles]]
== The Four Principles

=== Scarcity and Opportunity Cost

A choice architecture exhibits _binding scarcity_ if, for at least one
resource _r_ and one decision node _d_, the set of actions affordable at _d_
is a proper subset of the set of actions available at _d_. The _opportunity
cost_ of a chosen action _a_ at _d_ is the value of the highest-ranked
alternative _a'_ foregone by choosing _a_, in the sense of Wicksteed (1910)
and Buchanan (1969).

In the game, binding scarcity is enforced at three levels:

. *Resource scarcity in the production phase.* A player's recipe set is, at
  most turns, infeasible to fire in full given the cubes held; the player
  must choose which recipes to activate.
. *Hex scarcity in the expansion phase.* The 7×9 hex map is exhausted in
  finite turns; eXpand actions race for the most productive biomes.
. *Action-budget scarcity per turn.* Each player has 2–3 actions per turn;
  the choice of which to spend is the per-turn opportunity-cost decision.

We claim that opportunity cost is _legible_ when, for any chosen action _a_,
the foregone alternative _a'_ is visibly representable on the player's board.
This is a design constraint, not a theorem: it is the principle that recipe
tiles, hex tiles, and action tokens be physically distinguishable so that
trade-offs are perceptible at a glance.

=== Information, Symmetry, and Fog

Following Aumann (1976), we distinguish _common knowledge_ (a fact known to
all players, known to be known by all, ad infinitum) from _private
information_ (held by a strict subset of players). Following Akerlof (1970)
and Stiglitz & Weiss (1981), we recognise that asymmetric information
generates inefficiencies — adverse selection, signalling costs, credit
rationing — that no efficient equilibrium can ignore.

In the game, the following are common knowledge:

* The boundary dashboard state.
* The set of recipes each player has unlocked (Schumpeterian innovation is
  not patentable in this game, by design — see <<sec_limitations>>).
* Hex ownership and infrastructure placement.
* Active trade agreements and their terms.

The following are private:

* Hand of unrevealed Crisis cards (when held privately).
* Hex contents under fog (eXplore is required to reveal them).
* Resource stocks (in some variants — playtest decision pending).
* Pact intentions prior to formal signing.

The fog-of-war mechanism is therefore a _structural_ rather than _stylistic_
feature: it is the game's enactment of the principle that information is
costly to acquire (Stigler 1961) and that this cost shapes equilibrium.

=== Factors of Production and Their Characteristic Returns

We adopt the canonical four-factor model:

[cols="1,2,1,3", options="header"]
|===
| Factor | Defining property | Return | Characteristic theory

| Land
| Inelastic supply; cannot be produced
| Rent
| Ricardo (1817); George (1879)

| Labour
| Time and effort of human persons
| Wage
| Smith (1776); Marshall (1890); Clark (1899)

| Entrepreneurship
| Bearing of uncertainty; novel combination
| Profit
| Knight (1921); Schumpeter (1934)

| Capital
| Produced means of production
| Interest
| Böhm-Bawerk (1889); Fisher (1930); Keynes (1936)
|===

Three theoretical claims attach to this taxonomy:

. *Rent arises from differential productivity.* For two land plots _L_₁ and
  _L_₂ with _L_₁ more productive, _L_₁ commands a positive rent above _L_₂'s
  zero-rent margin (Ricardo 1817, Ch. II).
. *Profit is the residual claimant.* After Rent, Wage, and Interest are paid
  to their respective factors, the remainder accrues to the entrepreneur as
  Profit; under perfect competition and full information, this residual
  vanishes (Knight 1921, Pt. III). True Profit therefore arises only under
  uncertainty.
. *Interest is the price of time preference.* Following Böhm-Bawerk (1889)
  and Fisher (1930), interest compensates the postponement of consumption.

The game's recipe model implements these as follows. Each recipe specifies
input cubes (Rent paid to Land, Wage paid to Labour, Interest implicit in
Capital infrastructure cost) and output cubes (Profit residual to the
operating player when the recipe is novel; commodity output otherwise). The
returns are not paid to separate "factor owners" — in a single-player
controlled-economy framing, the player owns all four factor positions — but
the structure of the recipe makes the contribution of each factor visible.

=== Externalities

Following Pigou (1920), an _externality_ is a cost or benefit accruing to a
party not party to the transaction that produced it. Following Coase (1960),
the efficient internalisation of externalities is sensitive to property-rights
allocation and transaction costs. Following Ostrom (1990), commons-pool
resources can be governed by neither pure markets nor pure states, but by
nested institutional arrangements with monitoring, sanctions, and
conflict-resolution mechanisms.

The game's central externality is the planetary-boundaries dashboard. Each
production recipe that emits pollution registers stress on a specific
boundary; the boundary's zone (Green/Amber/Red/Black) is common knowledge.
The dashboard is therefore the game's _Pigouvian register_: it makes the
external cost of every recipe firing publicly visible and quantitatively
attributable.

The game does not impose a Pigouvian _tax_ — there is no automatic levy on
emissions. Instead, the externality manifests through the crisis-card draw
formula

[stem]
++++
N_t = \left\lceil 1 + 0.5 a_t + r_t + 2 b_t \right\rceil, \quad N_t \in [1, 5]
++++

where stem:[a_t, r_t, b_t] are the counts of Amber, Red, and Black boundaries
at the start of turn stem:[t]. Polluting players raise stem:[N_t], drawing
crisis cards on themselves and on every other player. This is a _Coasean_
externality without internalisation by default; the game's diplomatic
mechanisms (eXchange, multilateral pacts) provide the _Ostromian_ governance
layer through which players may collectively internalise.

[[sec_production]]
== The Four-Factor Production Model

We model production as a Leontief technology (Leontief 1941) with binary
activation. Let stem:[\mathcal{R}] denote the set of recipes available in the
current era, and stem:[\mathcal{F} = \{\text{Land, Labour, Capital, Knowledge}\}]
the factor index set. (We discuss Knowledge's mapping to Entrepreneurship in
<<sec_mapping>>.) Each recipe stem:[r \in \mathcal{R}] is characterised by an
input vector stem:[\mathbf{i}_r \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}^{|\mathcal{F}|}] and
an output vector stem:[\mathbf{o}_r], with possible non-zero externality
component stem:[\mathbf{e}_r] indicating boundary stress emitted.

Player stem:[i] holds factor stocks stem:[\mathbf{x}_{i,t}] at the start of
turn stem:[t]. The activation indicator for recipe stem:[r] under player
stem:[i] is

[stem]
++++
\alpha_{i,r,t} = \begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } \mathbf{x}_{i,t} \geq \mathbf{i}_r \text{ and player elects to fire} \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
++++

The post-production stock is

[stem]
++++
\mathbf{x}_{i,t}^+ = \mathbf{x}_{i,t} + \sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}_i} \alpha_{i,r,t}(\mathbf{o}_r - \mathbf{i}_r)
++++

and the externality contribution to the boundary register is
stem:[\sum_r \alpha_{i,r,t} \mathbf{e}_r]. Recipes the player owns but cannot
or chooses not to fire contribute zero. This formulation has three desirable
properties for board-game implementation:

. *Atomicity.* Each recipe either fires or does not; there are no fractional
  outputs.
. *Local computability.* Activation depends only on the player's own stocks
  and decisions; no global price vector need be solved.
. *Visible scarcity.* The set of unfired recipes is precisely the set of
  binding-scarcity decisions made this turn.

The Leontief specification is appropriate to board-game discreteness in a way
that the more general Cobb–Douglas form stem:[Y = AL^\alpha K^\beta] is not:
the latter requires fractional inputs and outputs and presumes smooth
substitution, neither of which is implementable with cubes on cardboard. We
note that the Leontief specification _excludes_ smooth factor substitution by
construction; we restore an analogue of substitution at the _recipe-choice_
level (a player may select between a Labour-intensive Farm recipe and a
Capital-intensive Mechanised Farm recipe) rather than at the marginal level.

[[sec_mapping]]
== The Resource-to-Factor Mapping

The game's design names five gameplay resources: Labour, Capital, Nature,
Knowledge, Energy. Reconciliation to the four-factor model requires two
non-trivial moves.

=== Nature ↔ Land

Nature is the game's Land factor under the ecological-economics rebranding of
Daly (1977), Georgescu-Roegen (1971), and Raworth (2017). The substantive
content is Ricardian: Nature is inelastically supplied, exhibits differential
productivity by biome, and accrues Rent to its holder. The terminological
substitution from "Land" to "Nature" is a design choice to foreground the
boundary-stress register in <<sec_externalities>>.

=== Knowledge ↔ Entrepreneurship

We adopt the Schumpeterian rather than Beckerian reading of Knowledge.
Becker (1964) and Mincer (1974) treat knowledge as _human capital_ — a stock
that augments Labour's marginal product and earns a wage premium.
Schumpeter (1934) treats it as the input to _innovation_ — the creation of
"new combinations" that earn temporary monopoly Profit until competitive
imitation erodes them.

The game uses the Schumpeterian reading: Knowledge is consumed in the
eXperiment phase to attempt breakthroughs (with stochastic success governed
by a d8 roll), and successful breakthroughs unlock new recipes that earn
Profit until they are open-sourced or imitated. Knowledge is therefore _the
factor that, when combined with uncertainty, becomes Entrepreneurship_. The
return to Knowledge in the game is Profit, not a wage premium.

=== Energy as Intermediate Good

Energy is _not_ a fifth factor. We treat it as a produced intermediate good
in the sense of Leontief (1941): a commodity that appears as the output of
some recipes (e.g., Coal Plant, Solar Plant) and as the input to others
(e.g., Factory, Lab). This is consistent with the standard treatment of
energy in input-output analysis and with the absence of energy from the
classical four-factor enumeration.

The cost of this treatment is that we cannot represent energy's
thermodynamic uniqueness — its irreversibility in the sense of Georgescu-
Roegen (1971) — at the production-function level. We restore this asymmetry
through the externality channel: energy production from fossil sources
emits Climate stress, while production from renewable sources does not.

The complete mapping:

[cols="1,1,1,2", options="header"]
|===
| Game resource | Factor | Return | Notes

| Nature
| Land
| Rent
| Hex-derived; biome-differentiated; non-replenishable except by recovery action

| Labour
| Labour
| Wage
| Population-derived; floors at 0 (social collapse)

| Capital
| Capital
| Interest
| Infrastructure-derived; compounds when reinvested

| Knowledge
| Entrepreneurship (when applied)
| Profit
| Stock under Becker; flow into novelty under Schumpeter; we use Schumpeter

| Energy
| —
| —
| Intermediate good; produced by some recipes, consumed by others
|===

[[sec_externalities]]
== The Planetary Boundaries Dashboard as Pigouvian Register

We specify the boundary dashboard as a public register of externality stress.
Let stem:[\mathcal{B} = \{B_1, \ldots, B_9\}] be the nine boundaries of
Rockström et al. (2009) and Steffen et al. (2015): Climate Change, Biosphere
Integrity, Land System Change, Freshwater Change, Biogeochemical Flows
(N/P), Ocean Acidification, Atmospheric Aerosol Loading, Stratospheric Ozone
Depletion, and Novel Entities. Let stem:[s_{j,t} \in \{0, 1, \ldots, 10\}]
denote the stress on boundary stem:[j] at time stem:[t]. Stress increments
arise from:

. *Recipe externality emissions* stem:[\mathbf{e}_r] (endogenous to player
  choice).
. *Cascade propagation* across linked boundaries (rule-determined; e.g.,
  Climate→Ocean, Land→Biosphere).
. *Crisis-card effects* (stochastic, drawn from era-stratified deck).

Stress decrements arise from:

. *Recovery actions* (player spends Capital and/or Knowledge to reduce
  stress on a chosen boundary).
. *Positive crisis events* (e.g., a Montreal-Protocol-analogue card in the
  Digital era).

The dashboard is _Pigouvian_ in that it makes external costs publicly
visible and attributable. It is _not_ Pigouvian in the standard textbook
sense of imposing a tax stem:[\tau = \text{MEC}] equal to marginal external
cost; the game has no centralised taxing authority. Instead, the externality
manifests through:

. The crisis-draw formula stem:[N_t], which raises crisis frequency for all
  players when stress is high (a _shared_ cost — itself an externality).
. Loss thresholds (4+ boundaries at Black ⇒ Planetary Collapse, all players
  lose), which impose a discrete catastrophic cost.
. Cooperative-victory thresholds, which require all-or-most boundaries in
  Green/Amber, providing a positive incentive for collective restraint.

This is closer to the _Ostromian_ commons-governance arrangement (Ostrom
1990) than to a Pigouvian tax: institutional rules and player coordination,
not a central planner, are the means of internalisation. We make this design
choice explicit because it foregrounds the diplomatic mechanism (eXchange)
as the primary lever rather than as colour.

[[sec_information]]
== Information, Symmetry, and the Fog Mechanic

The fog mechanic is the game's enactment of costly information acquisition
in the sense of Stigler (1961) and the Hayekian dispersion of knowledge
(Hayek 1945). At game start, hexes outside each player's starting region
are face-down. The eXplore action reveals one or more adjacent hexes at a
cost (one action token, possibly Knowledge expenditure). Information
acquired is privately held by default and may be made common knowledge
through eXchange (sharing maps under a trade agreement) or through eXpand
(visible movement onto a hex reveals its contents to all observers).

The game-theoretic significance of fog is that it creates _credible scope
for surprise_: a player may exit fog with a configuration of resources
unanticipated by others, generating the Knightian uncertainty that makes
genuine Profit possible (cf. Knight 1921, Pt. III; Schumpeter 1934). Without
fog, the game collapses to a perfect-information game in which optimal
strategies are (in principle) computable, and the Schumpeterian Profit
channel evaporates.

We note that fog is _asymmetric_: each player has private information about
their own explored area. This is not the symmetric incomplete information of
a typical Bayesian game (Harsanyi 1967), but a true asymmetric-information
setting. Lemons-style adverse selection (Akerlof 1970) is therefore in
principle possible — a player may offer to trade resources from a hex of
which the buyer knows little — and is a designed-in feature of the eXchange
phase. Trade-card text should accordingly include _due-diligence_ provisions
(see <<sec_pillars>>).

[[sec_emergence]]
== The Emergence of Stability

We confront a foundational design choice: should the game compute a
stability index, or should stability emerge?

The Walrasian tradition (Walras 1874; Arrow & Debreu 1954) treats equilibrium
as the simultaneous solution of all market-clearing conditions; in its
fictional implementation an _auctioneer_ calls out price vectors until
markets clear (_tâtonnement_). The Hayekian critique (Hayek 1945) is that
no such auctioneer exists in actual markets, and equilibrium emerges from
local agents responding to local information through the price system.

Daskalakis, Goldberg & Papadimitriou (2009) showed that computing a Nash
equilibrium is PPAD-complete; no polynomial algorithm is known. Asking four
players around a table to solve for a Nash equilibrium each turn is
therefore not merely impractical but theoretically infeasible.

We adopt the Hayekian commitment as a design axiom:

[NOTE]
====
*Axiom (Emergent Stability).* The game shall maintain no separate stability
index, harmony score, or computed equilibrium statistic. Stability shall be
read off the existing rule mechanisms: the boundary dashboard, the active-
versus-discarded pact ledger, the Labour floors, and the next-turn crisis
draw count. If stability is not visible from these signals, the rule system
is under-specified, not under-instrumented.
====

This axiom has three consequences for the design:

. *No "Stability Index" mechanic.* Earlier drafts of the storyboard
  proposed a weighted-sum index. This is rejected on Hayekian grounds.
. *No "Temperature Phase".* Earlier drafts proposed a player-rating
  mechanism with harmonic-mean aggregation. This is rejected for the same
  reason: it is a Walrasian auctioneer in a different costume.
. *Cooperative endings are gated on observable conditions.* The
  Post-Scarcity Utopia, Harmonic Degrowth, Digital Commons, and Managed
  Decline endings each specify their conditions in terms of dashboard
  state, infrastructure, and player survival — quantities already on
  the board.

The game-theoretic claim — that the cooperative-ending boundary states are
approximate Nash equilibria of the underlying repeated game — is to be
established offline by the design team using a payoff-matrix analysis (see
ROADMAP item ADR-002), not at the table.

[[sec_pillars]]
== Binding to the 6X Action Pillars

Production does not occur in a vacuum. Each recipe-firing presupposes a
history of actions that established the necessary inputs. We therefore bind
each recipe activation to a precondition over the 6X pillar action history:

[cols="1,2,2", options="header"]
|===
| Pillar | Generates | Recipe precondition supplied

| eXplore
| Knowledge of map; reveals hexes
| Visibility of biome (required to know which recipes are even applicable)

| eXpand
| Hex ownership; Land-factor access
| The Land input (a recipe cannot fire on an unowned hex)

| eXploit
| Recipe firings (production phase itself)
| The activation event itself

| eXterminate
| Denial of inputs to rivals
| Disables a rival's recipes (e.g., trade-route blockade reduces input flow)

| eXchange
| Re-routes inputs and outputs across players
| A player's surplus may satisfy another's input requirement

| eXperiment
| Unlocks new recipes
| Recipe membership in stem:[\mathcal{R}_i] is itself a function of
  prior eXperiment activity
|===

A recipe tile is therefore not freestanding furniture on a player board; it
is a contract between a 6X action history and a production output. Visually,
this can be made legible by colour-coding recipe-tile preconditions to the
6X pillar that supplied them.

We note that this binding closes a potential exploit in earlier drafts: a
player could not, under the bound model, suddenly fire a Factory recipe in
the first turn merely because they have the cubes. The Factory recipe
requires (i) a hex with appropriate terrain (eXplore + eXpand), (ii) the
recipe template to be in the player's set (eXperiment in some variants;
era-default in others), and (iii) the input cubes (the production
precondition). All three preconditions are independently checkable on the
board.

[[sec_limitations]]
== Limitations and Open Questions

We declare the following limitations of the present formalisation. Each is
a candidate for resolution through playtesting or further design work.

. *Single-player controlled-economy framing.* We treat each player as the
  sole owner of all four factor positions in their region. We do not model
  intra-civilisation factor markets (workers, landlords, capitalists, and
  entrepreneurs as distinct agents). This is a deliberate simplification;
  expansions could introduce role cards (e.g., the existing Gandhi card
  approximates a labour-coalition model) that partially restore this
  structure.
. *No money, no prices.* The game uses cube barter rather than a numeraire
  good. This sacrifices the Walrasian-price-vector channel but greatly
  simplifies bookkeeping. A future expansion could introduce a Currency
  resource as a Hicksian numeraire (Hicks 1939), with attendant inflation
  mechanics.
. *Recipe novelty is non-rivalrous and non-patentable.* Once a recipe is
  unlocked by any player, design intent is that other players may unlock
  it through their own eXperiment actions, but a player cannot prevent
  another's eXperimentation. This is a deliberate departure from real
  intellectual-property regimes; the design choice favours legibility and
  short rules over IP-system simulation.
. *The Profit residual is implicit, not explicit.* The four-factor returns
  (Rent, Wage, Profit, Interest) are not paid out as separate cube types in
  the current model; they are subsumed in the recipe input/output structure.
  A future iteration could introduce explicit factor-return tokens, at the
  cost of additional component complexity.
. *Asymmetric information equilibria are not solved.* Our claim that
  cooperative endings are approximate Nash equilibria of the repeated game
  presumes a tractable payoff-matrix model that has not yet been
  constructed (see ROADMAP ADR-002). Until that model exists, the claim is
  a design conjecture, not a theorem.
. *Boundary cascade weights are not calibrated.* The cascade graph topology
  is given; the cascade weights (how much stress propagates from one
  boundary to another) are stipulative. Calibration against the
  Earth-system literature (Steffen et al. 2015; Lenton et al. 2008) is
  required and is non-trivial.
. *Card text replaces historical flavour with mechanical/strategic cue text
  by design decision (2026-04-30).* Cards do not carry historical analogues
  in their flavour text; they carry mechanical mnemonics or strategic
  prompts. The educational-historical content lives in the rulebook
  appendix and the supplementary educational supplement, not on the cards
  themselves.
